# Osis

## Product Management for the Agentic Coding Era

*A Manifesto*

---

*Osis — March 2026*

## I. The Problem

The best products aren't built by better engineers. They're built by teams with product clarity — a shared understanding of what they're building, why it matters, and what to do next.

For decades, this clarity was the domain of product managers. PMs at the best companies in the world — the ones shipping products people actually love — run a process: structured specs, clear priorities, deliberate decisions. It's not magic. It's discipline. And it works.

But most builders don't have access to that process. Solo founders, small teams, indie hackers, agency developers — they skip straight to code. Not because they don't care about product thinking, but because no one ever gave them the tools to do it. Product management has been locked inside organizations that can afford dedicated PMs, expensive tooling, and formal processes.

So they vibe code. They build on gut feel. They start with the login page, then the dashboard, then whatever feels right next. There are no specs. No priorities. No system of record for what the product is or where it's going. When things break — and they always break — there's nothing to go back to. No source of truth. Just a pile of code and a fading memory of the original idea.

This is the hidden curriculum of building products. You can learn to code from anywhere. But learning to think like a product builder? That knowledge has been trapped in the hallways of companies most people will never work at.

---

## II. The Deeper Structure

The problem has two layers, and they reinforce each other.

**Layer 1: The knowledge gap.** Product management is an oral tradition. The best PMs learned by sitting next to other great PMs. The frameworks exist in blog posts and books, but reading about structured product thinking and actually doing it are completely different things. Information doesn't translate to execution. Knowing that you should write a spec is useless if you don't know how to write one that actually drives decisions.

**Layer 2: The context gap.** Even when someone does the product work — writes specs, makes decisions, defines priorities — that context evaporates. It lives in Notion docs nobody reads, Slack threads that scroll away, meetings nobody recorded. The specs drift from reality. The decisions lose their rationale. New team members (human or AI) start from zero every time.

These layers compound. The builder who doesn't know how to do product work produces no artifacts. The builder who does know produces artifacts that decay. Either way, the result is the same: the codebase has no product memory. Every decision is made in a vacuum.

And now, with AI agents writing production code, the stakes are higher than ever. An agent without product context doesn't just build slowly — it builds the wrong thing, confidently, at scale.

---

## III. The History of Attempts

Every generation of tooling has tried to close some version of this gap. Each captured something real. None solved the structural problem.

| Era | Approach | What It Captured | Structural Limitation |
|-----|----------|------------------|-----------------------|
| Wikis & docs | Confluence, Google Docs, Notion | Writing things down matters | Docs are artifacts, not living systems. They decay the moment they're written. |
| Project management | Jira, Asana, Linear | Work needs tracking and structure | Tracks tasks, not product thinking. You can have a perfect sprint board and still build the wrong product. |
| Product platforms | Productboard, Aha!, Coda | Product decisions need a dedicated home | SaaS silos. Context lives in the tool, not the codebase. Expensive. Designed for established teams, not solo builders. |
| Templates & frameworks | PRD templates, OKR frameworks, Notion templates | Structured thinking can be packaged | A blank template doesn't teach you how to think. It just gives you empty boxes to fill. |
| AI writing tools | GPT wrappers, PRD generators | AI can draft documents fast | Generates text, not understanding. A generated PRD that nobody internalizes is worse than no PRD — it creates false confidence. |

The pattern: every tool either helps you write things down (but they decay), helps you manage work (but not product thinking), or lives in a silo (disconnected from where the code actually gets built).

None of them made product management a native part of building software.

## IV. What Changed

Three shifts happened simultaneously, and their intersection creates an opening that didn't exist before.

**AI agents write production code.** This is no longer theoretical. Agents are building real features, making architectural decisions, and shipping to production. But agents are only as good as their context. An agent with clear specs builds the right thing. An agent without them hallucinates product decisions. The demand for structured product context just went from "nice to have" to "critical infrastructure." The era of vibe coding is giving way to spec-driven development — where what you build is defined before the first line of code is written.

**The CLI skill as distribution model.** shadcn proved that you can distribute software as files you install into your codebase — no SaaS, no vendor lock-in, no dashboard. You own the code. This model works for product specs too. Install a skill, get a protocol, own your data. The distribution mechanism for product management tooling no longer requires a platform.

**More people can build than ever before.** AI lowered the barrier to writing code dramatically. The bottleneck shifted. The scarce resource is no longer engineering capacity — it's product clarity. Millions of new builders can now ship code but have never been taught how to think about what to build. The market for democratized product management just exploded.

## V. The Vision

**Osis is product management as an open protocol, maintained through conversation with an AI agent, living in your codebase.**

Stop vibe coding. Build like the best teams in the world.

The protocol is a structured hierarchy of specs — a system of record for what your product is (the digital twin) and what it's becoming (the spec chain). The agent skill is the interface — you don't fill out templates, you have conversations, and the protocol writes itself.

One is incomplete without the other. The protocol without the agent is just more templates to ignore. The agent without the protocol is just another chatbot. Together, they are a product process that runs itself.

Zero learning curve. Maximum leverage. You don't watch YouTube videos, read articles, or configure anything. You install the skill and it handles the rest. That's the promise — the install itself is the smart move.

Install it. Talk to it. Get specs that tell you exactly what to build. Then build.

The conversation isn't delaying code. It's writing the blueprint that makes the code go faster.

---

## VI. How It Works (Conceptual)

Osis operates on a duality: **what IS** and **what's BECOMING**.

**The Digital Twin** is the product compressed to its essence. A mechanical description of what the codebase produces today — every system, every connection, every capability. Present tense. No vision, no aspiration. Just truth. It's the first thing an agent reads before touching your code.

**The Protocol** is the spec chain — vision, product spec, phase plans, system specs, implementation specs. Each level answers a different question, from "why does this exist?" down to "what does the agent build next?" Specs flow downward. Changes propagate upward. Nothing drifts because the agent maintains coherence.

The gap between the twin and the protocol is the work. The twin says "here's what we have." The protocol says "here's where we're going." The delta is your roadmap, made concrete.

```
Twin (what IS) ←→ Gap = the work ←→ Protocol (what's BECOMING)
```

The user experiences none of this complexity. They just have a conversation. The agent handles the protocol mechanics — creating specs, maintaining consistency, detecting drift, propagating changes. You talk about your product. Osis turns that into a structured system of record that any human or AI can read and build from.

**Install → Talk → Specs → Build.**

## VII. The Impact

**For the solo founder** who has been building for three months and still can't explain what their product does in one sentence: thirty minutes with Osis produces a vision spec and product spec that crystallize the idea. The code they write after that conversation is different. It has direction.

**For the small team** that keeps rebuilding features because nobody remembers why they were built that way: Osis gives them a system of record. Decisions have rationale. Specs have history. A new engineer reads the twin and understands the product in minutes, not weeks.

**For the AI-native builder** who uses agents for everything: Osis is the missing context layer. Their agents stop hallucinating product decisions and start building against real specs. The code quality goes up not because the agent got smarter, but because it finally knows what it's building.

**For the ecosystem:** Product clarity becomes a property of the codebase, not the team. When the founder leaves, the knowledge stays. When the team grows, alignment scales. When you hand your repo to an agent, it doesn't start from zero.

The before and after is stark:
- **Before:** Vibe coding. Building on gut feel. No specs, no process, no alignment.
- **After:** Spec-driven building. Product clarity from day one. Building like the best teams in the world.

## VIII. The Long Horizon

One day, whenever you have an idea and spin up a repo, you install Osis too. Before you write a line of code, you get product clarity. It's as natural as initializing git.

The protocol becomes a standard — not because anyone mandates it, but because it works. Open source projects ship with an `osis/` folder. Investors ask for the twin. Agents expect structured specs. Product discipline stops being a competitive advantage of well-funded teams and becomes a baseline expectation of any serious project.

Osis elevates any project to professional product management. From the college student building their first app to a founder in an office — they all now have elite product management capability for their project.

Osis doesn't replace product managers. It makes product thinking accessible to everyone who builds. The best PMs in the world still do work that no protocol can capture — taste, judgment, political navigation, user empathy at depth. But the structural work — the specs, the priorities, the system of record — that becomes infrastructure. Available to everyone. Maintained by agents. Owned by builders.

The long-term goal is a system that guides you from idea to PMF — the full product lifecycle, not just the build phase. Ideation, validation, building, launch, growth, and product-market fit. One protocol, one conversation at a time, all the way through.

The logical terminus: **product management as a solved distribution problem.** The knowledge exists. The frameworks exist. The gap was always distribution — getting structured product thinking into the hands of every builder, at the moment they need it, in the place where they work. Osis closes that gap.
