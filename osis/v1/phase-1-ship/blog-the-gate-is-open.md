**Post 1: The Product Clarity Gap**

We automated the wrong side of building software.

Over the last two years, AI has made code generation nearly free. A solo founder can ship what used to take a team of ten. A prototype that took a quarter now takes a weekend.

But here's the data nobody's sharing: the failure rate hasn't changed.

Products still fail at the same rate they always have. Not because of bad code — because of bad direction. We gave builders 10x speed on execution and 0x improvement on knowing what to execute.

The bottleneck has shifted. The scarce resource is no longer engineering capacity. It's product clarity — the structured understanding of what you're building, for whom, and why it will win.

The companies that consistently ship products people love — the Stripes, the Linears, the Notions — they all share one thing: a rigorous product process. Structured specs, deliberate decisions, a system of record for product intent. This process has historically been locked inside organizations that can afford dedicated PMs and formal processes.

As code generation becomes commoditized, product direction becomes the entire competitive advantage. The builders and teams that develop product clarity — regardless of their size or background — will outperform those who build faster but aim worse.

We're calling this shift "spec-driven development" — the practice of defining what you build before you build it, maintaining living specs that guide both human and AI builders, and systematically closing the gap between product intent and product reality.

This isn't new. The best teams have always worked this way. What's new is that it's now accessible to everyone.

**Post 2: The Distribution Problem**

Product management has a distribution problem.

The frameworks exist. Amazon's Working Backwards. Apple's product discipline. JTBD. The Double Diamond. Decades of hard-won knowledge about how to build products people love.

This knowledge is documented in books, blog posts, and internal wikis. But documentation alone has never been the issue.

The issue is operationalization. Reading about product management and actually doing product management are completely different skills. A blank PRD template doesn't teach you how to think. A Notion page about Working Backwards doesn't help you actually work backwards.

This is why product management has historically spread through apprenticeship — PMs learning from other PMs, absorbing judgment and process through proximity. It's an oral tradition that scales poorly.

AI agents change this equation fundamentally. An agent can embody a product process — not just describe it, but actively facilitate it. Ask the right questions. Challenge assumptions. Surface contradictions. Write structured specs. Maintain consistency over time.

The distribution mechanism for elite product thinking is no longer "hire a PM who worked at a company that does this well." It's "install a skill that carries the process."

We're working on this at Osis — an open protocol for product management that lives in your codebase, maintained through conversation with an AI agent. But the insight is bigger than any one tool: the next generation of product management won't be taught in classrooms or learned on the job. It will be distributed as software.

**Post 3: Spec-Driven Development**

There's a new methodology emerging and it doesn't have a name yet. Let me propose one: spec-driven development.

The core idea: in an era where AI agents write production code, the specs become the product. The code is the output. The specs are the input that determines whether that output is valuable or worthless.

How it works:

1. Before you code, you define product intent in structured specs — what you're building, for whom, and why
2. Specs flow down as constraints — from vision to product spec to system specs to implementation specs
3. AI agents build against these specs, not against vague prompts
4. Discoveries during building flow back up, updating specs
5. The specs are living documents maintained by agents, not static artifacts that decay

This is how the best product teams have always worked — just manually. The innovation isn't the process. It's making the process executable by agents and accessible to everyone.

The implications:

- Code quality improves not because agents get smarter, but because they finally know what they're building
- Product knowledge becomes a property of the codebase, not the team — when someone leaves, the context stays
- The gap between "what we intended" and "what we built" becomes measurable and systematically closeable
- Any builder, regardless of background, can operate with the product rigor of a well-funded team

Vibe coding was the first era of AI-assisted development. Spec-driven development is the second. The builders who make this transition will build products that last.
