# linear method

There is a lost art of building true quality software. To bring back the right focus, here are the foundational ideas Linear is built on.

This compilation gathers the text from each page of Linear’s Method as of October 2025.  The content is organized under each of the main topics and pages within the Linear Method.  These excerpts highlight the key principles, practices and guidelines Linear uses to build quality software.  For source verification, reference the cited lines.

Introduction – Principles & Practices

Linear’s core principles emphasize building tools for creators, ensuring the software is purpose‑built, fostering momentum rather than sprinting, maintaining a meaningful direction, and aiming for clarity by avoiding unnecessary terminology ￼.  The company encourages teams to say no to busy work and to design software that works for the user rather than forcing users to work around the tool; simplicity comes first with power and flexibility growing as needed ￼.  Decisions should be made efficiently and then teams should move on ￼.

Under practices, Linear recommends setting strategic product initiatives that are ambitious yet flexible; these initiatives help connect daily work to larger goals and should be reviewed regularly ￼.  The company suggests working in n‑week cycles (two‑week cycles are common) to create a healthy routine and focus, with manageable backlogs that don’t save every low‑priority request ￼.  Product teams should mix feature and quality work, define clear project and issue ownership, and write concise project specifications ￼.  Understanding users through feedback is critical: feedback should be attached to relevant issues so that customer voices inform development ￼.  Issues should be scoped as small as possible, progress measured by actual work, and teams should run cross‑functional with designers and engineers collaborating closely ￼.  Writing changelogs is encouraged to track progress internally and communicate externally ￼.

Direction – Setting the Product Direction

Setting a clear product direction aligns the entire organization toward shared goals.  A well‑defined direction aids daily decision‑making and helps teams prioritize projects ￼.  Initiatives are a key tool: by mapping out the product journey, a company articulates a vision and sets an execution path that is slightly beyond reach; anyone can consult these initiatives to understand priorities and progress ￼.  Whether a startup or a large company, clear product plans keep teams aligned, encourage ambitious timelines and foster transparency ￼.

Set Useful Goals

Startups move quickly, and it may be difficult to know what to work on next.  Setting medium‑ to long‑term goals reminds teams what truly matters ￼.  Even without historical data or many users, it’s important to choose goals that propel the company forward.  When starting from zero, think in incremental steps (e.g., getting your first user, then ten users, then generating revenue) ￼.  Successful companies often begin with small achievements, iterate, and scale over time ￼.

Prioritize Enablers and Blockers

Effective prioritization requires understanding enablers (features that add value) versus blockers (gaps that prevent users from using the product).  With limited resources, teams must focus on the features that move the needle soonest ￼.  It’s crucial to discern whether a problem truly prevents usage or is a nice‑to‑have; growth comes from investing in the right enablers and removing critical blockers ￼.  Timing also matters: prioritize work that advances immediate goals and consider the compounding effects of building certain features now versus later ￼.  Linear’s example of launching with only Google login illustrates prioritizing speed over completeness; more authentication methods came later ￼.

Scope Projects Down

For early‑stage projects, scope is especially important.  Linear advises designing projects that a small team (one to three people) can complete in one to three weeks; minor fixes should take only hours or a day ￼.  Shorter projects encourage continuous shipping and quick feedback loops, while smaller teams reduce communication overhead ￼.  Because early on it’s difficult to predict impact, avoid massive projects; if a project can’t be scoped down, break it into stages ￼.  Linear shipped early versions of Cycles and Projects within two weeks, collected feedback immediately, and iterated—these features later became major product components ￼.

Building – Generating Momentum

Linear encourages teams to generate momentum by taking swift action and making progress every day ￼.  Instead of deliberating too long, decide whether to pursue a task and execute it promptly.  Even when the most important task isn’t obvious, teams should act—perhaps by talking to users or performing smaller tasks to gain clarity ￼.  Startups rarely fail because of too much progress; they fail when they move too slowly or give up ￼.

Write Issues Instead of User Stories

Linear considers user stories outdated and advocates writing short, plain‑language issues ￼.  A good issue clearly communicates a task so the assignee can execute it while providing enough context for others.  User stories, a practice from decades ago, can waste resources by obscuring tasks and creating unnecessary complexity ￼.  Modern teams understand customers directly; user stories are often cargo‑cult rituals that restrict engineers to mechanical roles ￼.  Instead, Linear suggests describing tasks directly and focusing discussions about user experience at the product level, not the task level ￼.

A Better Way to Write Issues
	•	Describe concrete tasks or problems: An issue should describe a clear deliverable—code, design, document or action. Non‑task ideas should be captured elsewhere ￼.  Placeholder issues can be created for exploration phases ￼.
	•	Write clearly and directly: Use succinct titles; descriptions are optional but can include context or links ￼.  When sharing user feedback, quote it directly and link to customer conversations ￼.
	•	Write your own issues: Everyone should write their own issues because it’s faster, forces deep thinking and allows for better problem solving ￼.  Even when writing issues for others (e.g., bug reports), frame them as problems and let the assignee propose solutions ￼.
	•	Keep user‑experience discussions at the product level: Discuss the overall user experience when planning projects; once tasks are delegated, teams with a deep understanding of users can deliver without detailed task‑level explanations ￼.  At Linear, projects often involve weeks of thinking before coding; once a plan is clear, the project owner delegates work and individuals write their own issues ￼.

Manage Design Projects

Linear manages design tasks within its own tool and stresses that designers and engineers collaborate closely.  Because design work can be unpredictable and hard to estimate, Linear outlines several stages for managing design projects:
	1.	Verifying the problem: Designers must confirm the problem exists and is worth solving ￼.  This may involve investigating surface‑level issues and identifying root causes.  Linear’s design team reviews feature requests from users and Slack communities and writes detailed project specs to think through problems ￼.
	2.	Exploration stage: Create a placeholder issue titled “Explore designs” and freely explore different options without judging feasibility ￼.  Exploration includes research and experimentation in Figma; the complexity of the problem dictates how long this stage takes ￼.  Feedback early in this stage is important; consider both general reactions and specific details, and ask why feedback is given ￼.  Provide context for the type of feedback sought ￼.
	3.	Choosing a direction: After exploration, pick a direction and identify the assets needed and discrete tasks to complete ￼.  Collaboration with engineers helps identify technical constraints and fosters shared understanding ￼.
	4.	Design and engineering handover: Linear works collaboratively; designers and engineers work together from spec writing through implementation.  Projects are shared between design and engineering teams; designers write their own issues and engineers write theirs, using sub‑issues when collaboration is needed ￼.
	5.	Personal workflow example: A designer might begin with exploration, share Figma screens or Loom videos for feedback, create specific tasks such as “Design X view,” and then hand off implementation sub‑issues to engineers ￼.

Build with Users

Early startups must learn what customers want.  Teams should seek feedback, iterate, and remain flexible ￼.  Founders must balance vision vs. feedback, refining product direction based on user insights without being excessively reactive ￼.  When users request features, focus on understanding the underlying problem rather than the requested feature ￼.  This approach helps determine whether a problem is valuable to solve and allows exploration of multiple solutions ￼.  Feedback should be gathered from the right users; listening to customers outside the target demographic may lead to misguided decisions ￼.  Use feedback to refine the product but rely on strategic initiatives to balance user needs with company goals ￼.

Launch and Keep Launching

Linear advises against waiting for a single major launch; instead, startups should launch multiple times ￼.  Large launches require significant preparation and carry more risk, whereas repeated launches build a narrative over time and compound interest ￼.  Early products aren’t ready for everyone, so it’s better to launch early, gather users and momentum, and iterate ￼.  Like changelogs, frequent launches remind the market of progress ￼.  Linear’s own launch history shows how multiple launches (e.g., announcing the company, raising seed funding, opening to everyone, raising Series A) cumulatively reach more people and accelerate learning ￼.

Build in Public

Showing what you’re building can be more beneficial than harmful.  Building in public may discourage competitors by demonstrating your speed and progress ￼.  Publishing a changelog is a practical way to build in public: it keeps the team aware of weekly achievements, reminds users that the product is improving, and shows investors consistent progress ￼.  When progress feels slow, looking back at the changelog highlights accomplishments ￼.  Building in public signals transparency and momentum, potentially attracting a community and discouraging competitors ￼.
